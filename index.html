I Opening
Case Study Papua New Guinea

A quiet institutional reform: how Papua New Guinea changed the way K1.165 billion a year is tracked, verified and used.

A GoPNG-led reform Jointly funded by PNG & Australia Delivered with Catalpa

Inside the Department of Implementation and Rural Development (DIRD), officers moved from sorting paper to reading the country. For the first time, government has a credible, national view of how district funds flow — and how they’re used. Reform didn’t come from a tool. It came from people, supported by one.

II Context
Background

Why SIP accountability matters.

The Service Improvement Program is the largest annual flow of development capital to Papua New Guinea’s provinces and districts. By formula, every district receives K10 million each year and every province another K10 million — money allocated for roads, classrooms, aid posts, courts and rural enterprise. For most districts, it is the largest single source of public investment they will see in a year.

The formula is the same from Port Moresby to Nuku — K10 million per district, every year. For most of that history, no national institution could say with confidence what most of it had bought.

This is where Papua New Guineans encounter the state — in the classroom roof that does or doesn’t get repaired, the aid post that does or doesn’t reopen, the road that does or doesn’t reach the highway in the wet. SIP now accounts for roughly half of all subnational capital spending.5 When the reporting on it fails, the failure shows up locally. That mandate has long been on paper. Until Bilum, the systems for exercising it credibly were not.

III The Challenge

A K9.65 billion blind spot.

Money disbursed. Money acquitted. Money traceable. By 2017, only the first was reliable.

For a Project Implementation Wing (PIW) officer at DIRD in 2019, the work began at the desk and ended in the box. Acquittals from districts across the country arrived as paper — by road, by air, in cardboard archive boxes that sat behind the desk waiting to be opened. There was no central database to consolidate them, query them, or cross-check them against the guidelines they were meant to satisfy. The volume rose. The visibility did not.

The credibility gap
  1. i

    In 2017, the auditor couldn’t sign off.

    The Auditor General’s Office could not form an opinion on the financial statements of any province or Local-Level Government in Papua New Guinea — a national-level qualification with no easy parallel in stable systems.1

  2. ii

    A failure rate that quadrupled in four years.

    In 2013, fewer than one in five provinces and districts failed to acquit SIP funding. By 2017, more than seven in ten did.1

  3. iii

    K9.65 billion disbursed. Some never accounted for.

    Between 2013 and 2022, K9.65 billion in SIP funds was disbursed across PNG. Two provinces and one district have never acquitted theirs.2

DIRD held the mandate. PIW officers held the work. What neither held was a system that could match the scale of either. The reform that followed didn’t begin with a tool. It began with that gap, named honestly, by the people closest to it.

IV Approach
The approach

At the speed of trust.

The pace was set by the relationship, not the schedule. Bilum moved when DIRD was ready to move. The team worked on the SIP cycle, not a delivery calendar. That choice — to let trust set the rhythm — shaped what got built, who built it, and who it was for.

Co-design sessions ran on the SIP cycle, not a project schedule. Decisions were made together, in DIRD’s offices.

For most of SIP’s history, acquittal was something districts owed Port Moresby. Bilum was built first to be useful at the front line — to district administrators planning a year, to PIW officers tracking a project, to subnational executives running a quarter. Reporting upward to DIRD became a by-product of locally useful work, not a tax on it.

With the data captured at source, DIRD’s role could change. Officers moved off the paperwork and into the oversight responsibilities the department had long held: compliance, quality assurance, and shaping the rules. The mandate had always been there. Bilum gave DIRD the tools to exercise it at national scale.

The delivery team was embedded inside DIRD — PNG-based staff working alongside Corporate Services, the Project Implementation Wing and the executive. By Phase 3, GoPNG cost-sharing exceeded K6 million across rollout travel and corporate support;6 the platform increasingly ran on government cycles, government decisions and government money.

V Timeline
Program timeline

A multi-year partnership.

Bilum was built across three phases. Phase 4 is now coming into view.

  1. Phase 1 Jun 2020 — 2021

    Concept & design

    Initial concept developed through human-centred design with DIRD officers, aligned to PNG-specific operational and governance needs.

  2. Phase 2 2021 — 2022

    Testing & development

    Platform development began alongside location-focused pilots that refined workflows, reporting mechanisms and system functionality.

  3. Phase 3 Jan 2023 — Dec 2025

    Scale & embed

    64 administrations onboarded, NEC-level endorsement secured, transition toward mandatory reporting for the 2025/26 cycle.

  4. Phase 4 2026 →

    Business as usual

    Nationwide mandatory reporting, expanded analytics, government-led operation, and in-house ICT capability inside DIRD.

VI In Use
Bilum in use

What the tool actually looks like.

A report that used to span multiple spreadsheets and days of back-and-forth now happens inside a single interface — from any district office with a connection.

Built for intermittent signal: submissions queue offline and sync when the line returns. One platform, four views — a national dashboard across 64 administrations, a guided submission flow from paper to evidence, analytics organised by district, sector and quarter, and a mobile view designed for the field.

VII The Shift
The transformation

A shift in the work itself.

The work changed at the desk. DIRD officers stopped retyping data and started questioning it. Reports moved from inbox to evidence; officers moved from clerk to analyst. The role of a Project Implementation Wing (PIW) officer in 2026 is recognisably different from the role in 2022.

That shift wasn’t an efficiency gain. It was a change in professional identity — from manual processing to strategic oversight. Officers had more reason to engage with data, more authority to act on it, and more standing within the department for doing so.

Transformation

From fragmented systems to unified oversight.

  • No centralised database for analysis

  • Mandate on paper, not exercised in practice
Then Now
After Bilum

  • 1,023 subnational users in total, including 204 with provincial- and district-level admin access; 205% growth in electorate-level unique users in twelve months6

Officers are using the platform. Sustaining that depends on the unglamorous work of timely account approvals, role permissions and structured onboarding — administrative, not technical.

On the gender gap: Female users currently make up 27%. Closing that gap is a stated Phase 4 priority — including outreach through provincial women’s offices and gender-balanced training cohorts.

On disability inclusion: Disability-disaggregated user data is not yet captured, and the platform has not been formally audited against WCAG accessibility standards. Both are Phase 4 priorities, alongside disability-inclusive training cohorts and accessibility improvements identified through user research. Silence on this is the wrong posture; the right one is to name the gap and the plan.

XII Embedding
System-level embedding

Institutionalising Bilum across government.

Phase 3 focused not only on rollout, but on embedding Bilum within government systems, structures and reporting expectations.

Policy

Government mandate

National Executive Council (NEC) Decision 217 of 2025 endorsed Bilum as a national platform and approved the legislative pathway to mandate it — giving the platform a policy foundation that outlasts any single program cycle.

Source: National Executive Council Decision 217/2025

Systems

Compliance and integration

The Department of Information and Communication Technology (DICT) issued the Certificate of Compliance to Bilum, confirming alignment with GoPNG ICT standards and policy. The Department of Finance (DOF) is integrating Bilum with the Integrated Financial Management System (IFMS).

Source: DICT certification · DOF integration workplan

Practice

Toward mandatory reporting

From the 2025/26 cycle, Bilum becomes the standard reporting pathway. Voluntary use is becoming required practice.

Source: Bilum Final Completion Report, Feb 2026

The handover

From program-led to government-led.

On 5 November 2025, the Bilum helpdesk was formally transferred to DIRD. The platform now runs from inside the department it was built to serve.

What started as a program is now a department’s practice.

XIII Confidence
Institutional change

Restoring confidence in DIRD’s role.

Bilum’s impact wasn’t confined to process. As the systems of reporting tightened, so did the credibility of the institution running them.

With Bilum in place, DIRD officers could monitor reporting in real time, contribute to national development dialogue, and exercise the oversight responsibilities the department had long held on paper but struggled to operationalise in practice.

XIV Relationships
Relationships

Changing relationships across the system.

DIRD’s relationship with subnational partners changed too. Provinces and districts increasingly trust and rely on Bilum for reporting and compliance, and engage DIRD as a partner rather than an auditor.

Reporting is a relationship. Inconsistent expectations corrode trust; consistent ones rebuild it. Bilum gave both sides the same shape to work to — from fragmented and frustrating interactions to a more professional, more accountable working rhythm.

XV Method
The method

Looking back at the way of working.

Three things mattered to how we worked. Not three things that worked — three things we’d insist on next time.

On the ground Every iteration of Bilum was tested against how the work actually happens inside DIRD.
  1. 01

    Institutional, not just technical.

    34 DIRD officers and 1,023 subnational users completed training; 19 DIRD officers reached competency against a co-designed framework.6 The helpdesk that runs the platform now sits inside DIRD — not in a vendor contract. We held the framework; DIRD held the work.

  2. 02

    Iterative and co-designed.

    132 platform changes shipped in Phase 3 alone — every one of them surfaced by user testing or PIW feedback.6 The submission flow released in 2025 is the third version of the one shipped in 2022.

  3. 03

    Embedded partnership.

    Catalpa staff sat inside DIRD across Phase 2 and Phase 3, working on the legal SIP cycle, not a vendor calendar. By Phase 3, DIRD officers led the rollout missions; Catalpa was beside them, not in front of them.

XVI Honestly
Honestly

What was harder than we expected.

Institutional reform is never linear. A few things slowed the work, shaped the approach, and will shape Phase 4.

  1. 01

    DIRD wasn’t in the room early enough.

    DIRD’s full participation in shaping the platform didn’t begin until 2023, when the rollout started. Phase 1 and Phase 2 features that PIW staff would have known to ask for had to be retrofitted later. Co-design works in proportion to who’s in the room — and we didn’t get that ratio right until Phase 3.

  2. 02

    The backend is still ours.

    DIRD officers run the front end with confidence. Backend engineering, hosting and major upgrades still sit with Catalpa. Closing that gap — building in-house ICT capability inside DIRD — is the work of Phase 4, not a closed chapter of Phase 3.

  3. 03

    Three clocks, not two.

    The program ran on a phase calendar. DIRD runs on the legal SIP cycle. Government data sharing runs on MoUs that took years to negotiate. The handover that closed Phase 3 was shaped less by our delivery rhythm than by all three of those clocks running at once.

  4. 04

    Continuity, not just compliance.

    NEC Decision 217/2025 endorsed Bilum, but ministerial transitions, senior turnover and political cycles are part of working in any government system. The platform also runs on government infrastructure that is itself maturing — connectivity, hosting, data security. Phase 4 includes building redundancy into both the technical and political layers; we don’t underestimate either.

None of these are failures. They are the texture of doing this work seriously. We share them because any reader planning similar reform deserves a realistic picture, not a marketing one.

XVII Legacy
Looking forward

The deeper impact.

Bilum showed what becomes possible when reform sits inside an institution, not on top of it.

It helped reframe expectations about what digital governance work could achieve in Papua New Guinea, and how it should be designed and delivered. That reframing had practical consequences inside the partnership: it shaped how the team approaches institutional strengthening, and contributed to a more grounded understanding of how digital systems can support public sector accountability — particularly in contexts where reform must run on government’s clock, not a delivery calendar.

In Nuku, Jillian Mase used to bind hundreds of pages of SIP reports into sector booklets, watch them set off on a seven-hour road trip to Wewak, then a flight to Port Moresby. They were lost in heavy rain. They were lost in staff turnover. Now an acquittal she uploads in Nuku is visible to a national administrator in minutes — traceable to a project, ready for analysis. Multiply that by 64 administrations. Multiply that by years. That’s what Bilum left behind.3
