Case Study Papua New Guinea

How Bilum helped people inside government do their jobs better.

Before Bilum, the challenge inside DIRD was not only administrative — it was deeply human. Staff were managing high volumes of SIP reporting through fragmented systems, inconsistent templates and manual processes that made analysis difficult and oversight harder still. The work was slow, frustrating and often overwhelming.

Bilum began to change that. This is the human story behind that change.

Scroll
Background

Why SIP accountability matters.

The Service Improvement Program is one of Papua New Guinea’s most significant sources of subnational development funding. At this scale, stronger accountability is critical to ensuring funds are tracked, reported and used effectively — helping government make better decisions and support improved service delivery.

Before Bilum, longstanding weaknesses in transparency, reporting and oversight limited credible visibility of SIP allocations, implementation and acquittals across the country. Developed through a close partnership between DIRD and Catalpa, Bilum did more than digitise reporting. It introduced clearer systems, better tools and more consistent workflows, while helping staff build confidence, strengthen relationships and take on a more strategic role in oversight and decision-making.

The challenge

Before Bilum.

SIP reporting was shaped by fragmented systems and uneven processes. Different offices used different spreadsheets and templates. Information arrived in large volumes, but there was no single system to consolidate it, interrogate it, or turn it into something useful for national oversight.

That absence of system had real consequences for the people doing the work. A PIW officer recalled the everyday reality behind weak processes — staff were not just slowed down, they were drained by them.

DIRD was seen as a sleeping giant — an institution with a substantial mandate and administrative authority, but without the tools, systems or coordination needed to exercise that role with confidence.

The approach

Building change with people, not around them.

One of the strongest features of the Bilum story is that the platform was not simply introduced into government from the outside. It was shaped with the people expected to use it. The case study attributes Bilum’s success in large part to its iterative development process, with district and national officers helping shape functionality based on practical needs and real operational experience.

Catalpa worked closely alongside DIRD throughout the process — as an embedded partner, not a distant contractor. That proximity helped build trust, responsiveness and shared ownership. The result was not only a better system, but a stronger sense that change was being made with government, not for government.

Co-design sessions across district and national offices informed every iteration of Bilum.
The transformation

A shift in the work itself.

Before Bilum, much of DIRD’s effort was absorbed by manual processing. After Bilum, staff increasingly shifted from data entry and document handling toward verification, analysis and strategic oversight.

This is more than an efficiency gain. It represents a change in professional role and value — from managing paperwork to using information more actively to support accountability and decision-making. Staff had more opportunity to develop new capabilities, engage with data more meaningfully, and contribute to a more evidence-based approach to oversight.

Institutional change

Restoring confidence in DIRD’s role.

The case study makes clear that Bilum’s impact was not confined to process improvement. It also helped restore visibility and confidence to DIRD’s mandate. As systems improved, so too did the department’s ability to play a more active and credible role in SIP oversight.

Bilum strengthened DIRD’s capacity to monitor reporting, contribute to national development dialogue, and exercise the oversight responsibilities it had long held on paper, but had struggled to fully operationalise in practice.

This is what makes Bilum such a compelling institutional story. It is not simply about digitising a reporting function. It is about helping a department step more fully into the role it was meant to play. A department once described as a sleeping giant began to look and act more like the institution its mandate required.

Relationships

Changing relationships across the system.

Bilum also appears to have changed the quality of interaction between DIRD and subnational partners. The platform improved relationships with subnational actors, who increasingly trust and rely on Bilum for reporting and compliance. It helped foster greater professionalism, consistency and accountability across those relationships.

Public sector systems are experienced relationally as much as technically. When expectations are inconsistent and reporting is unclear, trust erodes. When systems become more consistent and more credible, relationships can begin to stabilise around a clearer shared process. Bilum has supported that shift — from fragmented and frustrating interactions toward a more trusted and professional reporting environment.

The method

More than a digital tool.

Three features explain why this work succeeded — and what digital reform in complex public sector environments really requires.

  1. 01

    Institutional, not just technical.

    The program understood that change would not come from technology alone, but from supporting institutional practices, roles and confidence. That meant working at the level of systems, people and process — not simply installing software.

  2. 02

    Iterative and co-designed.

    Bilum was developed iteratively and collaboratively with government users. That approach helped ensure the platform fit the reality of DIRD’s operations, and fostered a sense of ownership and trust that was critical to sustained use.

  3. 03

    Embedded partnership.

    Catalpa worked closely alongside government throughout — as an embedded partner, not a distant contractor. That proximity and responsiveness helped shape a more effective, more contextually grounded result.

Looking forward

The deeper impact.

Bilum demonstrated what is possible when reform efforts are built around people, systems and institutions — not simply tools.

It helped reframe expectations about what digital governance projects could achieve in Papua New Guinea, and how they should be designed and delivered. That reframing had practical consequences: it informed subsequent governance platforms, shaped new approaches to institutional strengthening, and contributed to a more grounded, more patient understanding of how digital systems can support public sector accountability.

In that sense, Bilum’s legacy is not only what it achieved inside DIRD. It is also what it helped others imagine about the role of digital tools in public sector reform — and how such reform might be pursued more effectively in the future.
